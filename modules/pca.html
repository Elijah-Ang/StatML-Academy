Dimensionality Reduction

    Principal Components Analysis

    The "Magic Shrink Ray" for high-dimensional data. Learn how PCA compresses many columns into a few components without losing the strongest patterns.

    Stage 01

    The Unsupervised Explorer

    In traditional "supervised" machine learning, we act like students with an answer key. We know what we want to predict (e.g., house prices) and use features to find the answer.

    Principal Components Analysis (PCA) belongs to unsupervised learning. Here, we are explorers. There is no answer key. We are handed a messy box of variables and asked to find the hidden structure.

    Imagine measuring students on a full performance sheet instead of only two numbers:

    60 rows x 6 columns

    The canvas will still use a 2D Speed-vs-Strength slice to build intuition, but the loadings, scores, and scree plot later on will come from the full generated dataset.

    Stage 02

    The "Too Much Information" Problem

    Why can't we just look at the variables one by one?

    Because dimensionality explodes fast. With 6 variables you already have 15 pairwise plots, and with 10 variables you jump to 45. No single view tells the whole story.

    If we only look at Speed (squashing the data to the horizontal axis), we lose all information about strength. The points overlap and hide the true shape of our students.

    If we only look at Strength (vertical axis), we lose speed. Neither axis alone captures the main "soul" of the dataset, because athletic students tend to be both fast and strong.

    When variables are correlated (moving together), keeping them separate is redundant. We need a way to combine them.

    Stage 03

    Finding the First Principal Component

    PCA looks for a brand new direction—a line sliced straight through the cloud of data.

    It rotates this line until the data points, when projected onto the line, spread out as much as possible.

    0.0
    Current Spread (Variance)

    The direction that creates the largest spread is called Principal Component 1 (PC1). By maximizing the spread, this line stays closest to the entire data cloud, giving us the clearest single summary.

    Stage 04

    The Secret Sauce: Loadings

    How does PCA actually draw this line? It acts like a "dimmer knob", mixing the original variables together using weights.

    These weights are called Loadings. They tell us how much each original variable contributes to the new component.

    PC1 Score = Σ(wⱼ × standardized featureⱼ)

    For this generated dataset, the PC1 loadings are computed directly from the same PCA fit used for the scores and scree plot:

    PC1 blends the columns that move together into one broader summary axis.

    Stage 05

    The Golden Rule: Scaling

    There is a massive trap in PCA. Because it hunts for maximum variance, it can be bullied by units.

    Even in this generated dataset, Strength naturally varies much more than Speed in raw units. Without scaling, PCA leans toward the higher-variance column; after Standardization, both columns get a fair vote.

    Step 1: Mean (μ) = Average of column
    Step 2: Center = X - μ
    Step 3: Variance (σ²) = Average of (X - μ)²
    Step 4: Std Dev (σ) = √Variance

    Step 5: Scaled (Z) = (X - μ) / σ

    Result: After scaling, every variable has a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1. Units disappear, and no single variable dominates just because it has bigger raw numbers.

    Stage 06

    Capturing the Leftovers: PC2

    PC1 captured the main trend (overall athleticism). But there is still some "thickness" left over in the cloud.

    To capture this, PCA finds a Second Principal Component (PC2). It follows the exact same logic, but with one non-negotiable rule:

    It must be exactly 90 degrees (perpendicular) to PC1.

    Because it is perpendicular, PC2 is completely uncorrelated with PC1. It captures the contrasts—for example, students who are stronger than expected for their speed.

    Stage 07

    The Transformation (Scores)

    Here is where the magic happens. We don't just draw lines on the old graph. We rotate the entire coordinate system.

    PC1 and PC2 become our brand new X and Y axes. The data points haven't changed their relationships to each other, but we are now viewing them from the most informative angle.

    Original matrix: 60 x 6
    PCA scores: 60 x 2

    Stage 08

    Proportion of Variance Explained

    How do we know if our components are actually useful? We look at the Proportion of Variance Explained (PVE) using a Scree Plot.

    Think of the total variation in the data as a pizza. PC1 always takes the biggest slice. PC2 takes the next biggest. The cumulative line shows how quickly we reach near 100% of the information.

    0%
    PC1 Variance
    0%
    Cumulative (PC1+PC2)

    Look for the "Elbow" in the plot. In datasets with 100 columns, you might find that just 2 or 3 components explain 95% of the variance. That's the "Magic Shrink Ray" at work.

    Synthesis

    The Best Summary

    PCA does not invent new data. It simply rotates your perspective to find the most meaningful angles.

    By finding directions that maximize variance, PCA allows us to squash datasets with hundreds of columns into just a few principal components, filtering out the noise while preserving the mathematical "soul" of the data.

    Remember:

    • Scale your data first.
    • Loadings are the weights.
    • Scores are the new coordinates.
    • Stop when the "pizza slices" of variance get too small.
