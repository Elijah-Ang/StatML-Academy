Stage 04

The Assumption of Stationarity

Many advanced statistical models require a series to be stationary before forecasting. A stationary series has statistical properties that do not change over time.

Stationarity Conditions
  • Constant Mean: The average does not drift up or down.
  • Constant Variance: The spread (volatility) stays consistent.
  • Constant Covariance: The relationship between past and present doesn't shift.

Watch the rolling moving average on the plot. If it climbs or falls significantly, the mean is not constant. This series violates the assumptions of basic autoregressive modeling and requires transformation.

Stage 05

Fixing It: Differencing

To stabilize the mean of a trending series, we apply differencing. Instead of looking at raw values, we analyze the change between consecutive months.

Y't = Yt - Yt-1

Differencing removes the trend, shifting the mean down to a stable baseline, making the series often closer to stationarity.

Stage 06

Looking to the Past (Lags & ACF)

A massive concept here is a lag—looking backward in time. Today's demand might be highly correlated with demand from exactly 12 months ago.

The Autocorrelation Function (ACF) mathematically measures how strongly a series is related to its own past values across different lags.

How to read this genuinely computed ACF: Bars extending outside the shaded region are statistically significant (p < 0.05). Notice the repeating spikes? Depending on the noise level, they often suggest a repeating seasonal structure.

Stage 08

Diagnostics: Evaluating Residuals

How do we know if a real-world forecasting model is adequate? We subtract the model's historical predictions from the actual raw data to check the residuals.

Residualt = Actual Yt - Predicted Yt
What Good Residuals Look Like
  • Centered around zero: No overall drift up or down.
  • Constant spread: Variance shouldn't wildly fan out.
  • No waves or patterns: All seasonality and trend should be captured by the model.
  • Occasional outliers: Random spikes are normal, but they shouldn't cluster.

Toggle the switch to see what happens when a model fails to capture the underlying seasonality. A robust model leaves residuals that closely resemble pure random noise.

Stage 10

Limitations & Caveats

Time series models fundamentally rely on the assumption that the future will structurally resemble the past. It is crucial not to overclaim the certainty of any forecast.

What Models Can't Do
  • Black Swan Events: Standard models cannot predict unprecedented external shocks (pandemics, sudden market crashes).
  • Structural Breaks: If a company pivots its business model at month 100, data from month 1 is no longer highly relevant.
  • Infinite Horizons: Prediction intervals eventually grow so wide that long-term forecasts become practically useless.
