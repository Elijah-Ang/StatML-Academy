Operational Guide

Polynomial Regression

From raw scatterplot to final equation. Learn the exact steps to expand features, evaluate errors, and select a model that actually generalizes.

Scroll to begin workflow ↓
Step 01

Inspect the Raw Data

What we do Plot the raw input against the response.
Why we do it To visually check the shape. A straight line will naturally fail to capture a curved dataset.
What this means If the data forms a hill, a valley, or an S-curve, simple math won't be enough.

Let's see what happens if we force a straight line anyway.

➔
Look at the black dots on the chart. Notice how they structurally form a curved shape rather than a straight path.
Step 02

Fit a Straight Line

What we do Fit a basic, flat or sloped straight line.
Why we do it To set a baseline and prove that standard linear regression fails on curves.
What this means The line completely ignores the true shape of our data. This failure is called underfitting.

We need to give the math the flexibility to bend.

➔
See the rigid blue line on the chart and the simple equation in the dashboard.
Step 03

Expand the Features

What we do Create new columns by squaring and cubing the input data.
Why we do it Adding powers of the input gives the underlying formula the ability to curve.
What this means First, we shrink the input numbers so squaring them doesn't create huge, unstable values. Then we build our Design Matrix.

Now the model has more ingredients to build with.

➔
Check the Design Matrix overlay. The model now sees the squared input as just another normal feature to learn from.
Step 04

Fit the Weights

What we do The algorithm calculates the best weights for each column in our matrix.
Why we do it To find the exact mathematical combination that fits the dots.
What this means The weights act like knobs that tell the curve how strongly to bend and in what direction.

Let's check how close our curve actually gets to the data.

➔
Look at the Weights overlay and the new equation in the dashboard. The curve now gracefully follows the data points.
Step 05

Compute Errors

What we do Measure the vertical distance between the true dots and our curve.
Why we do it To see exactly where the model is still wrong.
What this means Small random misses are normal, but a clear pattern in the misses means the model is still failing.

Let's see what happens if we add too many curves.

➔
See the dashed red lines and the Residual Plot. The Train MSE above scores how well the model fits the data it saw.
Step 06

Check for Overfitting

What we do Push the model to extreme complexity and test it on hidden data.
Why we do it To catch overfitting before we use the model in the real world.
What this means Training error can look amazing while test error gets much worse, which is the warning sign of overfitting.

We need to find the balance between too simple and too complex.

➔
Look at the Validation Curve. Training error drops, but Test error explodes. The wiggly curve completely misses the new gold dots.
Step 07

Find the Sweet Spot

What we do Test different complexities to find the lowest Test Error.
Why we do it The best shape changes depending on the data.
What this means We want the simplest curve that generalizes well to new situations.

Play with the controls until you find the perfect balance.

➔
Change the Dataset Shape, then use the slider to explore. Find the simplest point on the Validation Curve before Test error rises.
Candidate Degree 2
Step 08

Lock in the Model

What we do Select the final model and lock in the formula.
Why we do it We only deploy a model after validation proves it works.
What this means We choose the simplest model that performs almost as well as the absolute best one.

This final equation is now ready for the real world.
