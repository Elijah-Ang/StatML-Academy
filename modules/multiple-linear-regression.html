Stage 01

The Multi-Variable World

Life is messy. If we want to predict a student's Test Score, knowing how many hours they studied is helpful, but it's rarely the full story.

What about how much sleep they got? What about the number of practice papers they completed? Multiple Linear Regression allows us to use several clues together to predict one number.

Stage 04

The Least Squares Method

Instead of guessing, the computer uses matrix algebra to instantly derive the absolute lowest possible Sum of Squared Errors directly from the dataset.

By minimizing the squared residuals across all 3 dimensions simultaneously, it settles perfectly into the optimal fit.

Why Square the Errors?
1. It stops positive (+4) and negative (-4) misses from canceling out to zero.
2. It heavily punishes huge misses (a miss of 10 costs 100 points), forcing the model to compromise evenly across all variables.
Stage 05

The Superpower: Holding Constant

This is the most critical concept in multiple regression. What does a Study Boost (β1) actually mean when we have an optimal fit?

It means: If a student studies one extra hour, their score increases by exactly that boost amount—assuming their sleep and practice papers stay exactly the same.

This logic applies to each predictor's coefficient: β1 is the effect of Study while Sleep and Practice are held fixed, β2 is the effect of Sleep while Study and Practice are held fixed, and β3 is the effect of Practice while Study and Sleep are held fixed.

The model does not literally freeze variables in real life—rather, it does this mathematically to estimate each variable's unique contribution.

Notice the highlighted slice on the plane. We've locked "Sleep" at 6 hours (and Practice at its average). As we move purely along the Study axis, the score rises in a perfect straight line determined solely by β1.

Score = β0 + β1(Study) + Locked
Stage 06

Building the Prediction

Now we transition away from the 3D plane. In multiple regression, the prediction is built from a base intercept plus separate contributions from each predictor.

Each predictor adds or subtracts its own specific amount. The final score is simply the sum of all these individual parts. Because of how the model fits the data, each of these contributions is added while the other predictors are treated as fixed.

Predicting a specific student

  • Study 4.0 hrs
  • Sleep 7.5 hrs
  • Practice 2 pps
How it stacks up:
  • • Base Score = Starting point
  • • Study Boost = β1 × Study hours
  • • Sleep Boost = β2 × Sleep hours
  • • Practice Boost = β3 × Practice papers
Final Score = Sum of all parts
Stage 07

The Spurious Trap

If multiple variables just stack, why not run a simple regression for each variable separately? Because of Spurious Correlations.

Imagine we want to predict Shark Attacks. If we run a simple model using Ice Cream Sales, the correlation is huge! It looks like ice cream causes shark attacks.

But the true driver is a lurking variable: High Temperature. Hot days cause both ice cream sales and beach visits (which lead to shark attacks).

The Power of "Holding Constant"
Multiple regression isolates a variable's unique contribution, which can reveal when a variable only looked important because it moved together with another predictor. If we put both Ice Cream and Temperature into the model, the model essentially compares days with the same temperature. Holding temperature constant, changing ice cream sales alone has almost zero unique effect on shark attacks.

If a coefficient shrinks close to zero after the other predictors are included, that suggests it has little unique relationship with the predicted outcome (y) after accounting for the others. This does not prove the variable has no real-world effect at all, only that it adds little unique information within this specific model.

Note: A larger coefficient doesn't always mean a "more important" predictor, as they depend on the unit of measurement.

Stage 08

The Adjusted R² Penalty

How do we know if our team of variables is actually good? R² tells us what percentage of the variance we explained.

But raw R² has a flaw: it almost never goes down when you add predictors. Adding any variable—even "Student's Shoe Size"—will mathematically force raw R² to increase, tricking you into thinking the model is better.

Adj R² = 1 - [ (1 - R²)(n - 1) / (n - p - 1) ]

Adjusted R² corrects for this by penalizing model complexity. In the formula, n is the number of observations and p is the number of predictors.

Every new predictor (p) uses up flexibility and increases the penalty. A new variable should only stay if it improves the model enough to justify its cost. A useless variable will cause Adjusted R² to stay flat or drop!

Stage 09

Reading the Model Output

When a data analyst fits a multiple linear regression, they don't look at a 3D plot—they look at a summary table to evaluate the results.

  • Estimate: The coefficient (β). How much the prediction changes for a one-unit increase in this predictor, holding all other predictors constant.
  • Std. Error: The uncertainty around the estimate. A smaller standard error means we are more confident in the coefficient.
  • t value: The estimate divided by its standard error. A larger absolute value (typically > 2) indicates strong statistical evidence that the true effect is not zero.

Note: Each coefficient should be read as "the change in predicted y for a one-unit increase in this predictor, holding the others constant." Because predictors can be measured in entirely different units, coefficients cannot be compared as pure "importance scores" unless the predictors are on comparable scales.

Multiple linear regression is a powerful detective. It models additive effects, isolates individual drivers, and finds the mathematically optimal fit by minimizing squared errors.
