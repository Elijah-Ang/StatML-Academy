Statistical Inference

The Chi-Square Test of Independence

A formal method for determining whether categorical variables are truly independent, or if a meaningful pattern exists within the counts.

Scroll to begin ↓
Stage 01

The Raw Counts

The Chi-Square test evaluates categorical data—data placed into distinct bins or groups without an inherent numeric value.

Imagine we survey 50 students to see if there is a relationship between two categorical variables: Club Choice (Art vs. Sports) and Favorite Snack (Apples vs. Chips).

Rule of thumb: Chi-Square works with frequencies (counts), not averages. Every individual belongs to exactly one cell in our table.
Stage 02

Hypotheses & Marginals

Are club choice and favorite snack related, or are they completely independent? We formalize this question using hypotheses.

H₀
Null Hypothesis: Club choice and favorite snack are independent. (No relationship).
H₁
Alternative Hypothesis: Club choice and favorite snack are not independent. (There is a relationship).

To test this, we must first look at the margins. The row totals and column totals tell us the overall preferences of the group, ignoring the split between the variables.

Stage 03

Expected Counts

If the Null Hypothesis is perfectly true, the counts inside the table should be directly proportional to the totals on the margins.

E = (Row Total × Column Total) / Grand Total

For example, if half of all students like apples (25/50), and there are 30 Art students, we would expect half of those Art students (15) to like apples if there is no special relationship.

Stage 04

Residuals & Contributions

The core intuition of Chi-Square is to compare what we Observed (O) with what we Expected (E). We square the residuals and divide by the expected count to scale the difference.

χ² = Σ [ (O - E)² / E ]
Interactive Matrix: Hover over any cell in the table to inspect its Observed, Expected, Residual, and individual χ² contribution.

We do this for every cell and add them all together to get our final test statistic. (Note: visual contributions are shown rounded to two decimals, but the true sum is roughly 8.33).

Stage 05

The Reference Distribution

To evaluate our test statistic of 8.33, we map it onto a Chi-Square distribution. The curve's exact shape is determined by the Degrees of Freedom (df).

df = (Rows - 1) × (Cols - 1) = 1

If our statistic lands past the critical boundary (here, 3.84 for α = 0.05), the deviation is too large to be attributed to random chance.

Stage 06

Reading the Output

Statistical software synthesizes these steps into a standard output summary. Here is how to read the final result:

Analysis Output
Pearson's Chi-squared test
X-squared 8.33
df 1
p-value 0.0039
alpha (α) 0.05
Decision Reject H₀
Effect Size (Phi) 0.41 (Moderate)
✓ Expected counts check All expected counts ≥ 5

Because the p-value (0.0039) is much smaller than our alpha threshold (0.05), we officially reject the null hypothesis. The Phi value of 0.41 confirms this is a moderate relationship.

Stage 07

Assumptions & Caveats

Before closing the book on this relationship, we must acknowledge the strict assumptions required for a valid Chi-Square test.

Required Conditions
  • Count Data: Measurements are frequencies, not means.
  • Independence: Each person belongs to only one category combination.
  • Sufficient Size: Ideally, all expected counts should be at least 5. If they are too tiny, the test breaks down.
Synthesis

We have strong statistical evidence that club choice and snack preference are connected. However, Chi-Square demonstrates association, not causation. We cannot declare that joining the Art club causes students to eat apples.
