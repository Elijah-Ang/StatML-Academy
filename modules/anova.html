Statistical Methods

Analysis of Variance

Are the differences between groups real, or are they just a product of everyday random noise? Discover how ANOVA decomposes variation to find the signal in the static.

Stage 01

The Raw Data

Imagine we are comparing test scores across three different teaching methods (Group A, B, and C).

If you look at the raw data in the visual panel, you'll notice that not everyone in Group A scored exactly the same. There is a natural spread, or noise, within every group. This is everyday biological, psychological, or measurement variation.

Stage 02

The Research Question

Our question isn't about individuals; it's about the group averages (the black lines).

Are the gaps between these group means large enough to confidently say the teaching methods have different effects? Or are the means just slightly off from each other by pure chance, drowning in the natural noise of the students?

Stage 03

The Rules of the Game

Before we build an ANOVA model, we must check our assumptions. If the data breaks the rules, standard ANOVA might mislead us.

Normality

Technically, ANOVA assumes the residuals (the error or distance of each point from its group mean) are normally distributed. In practice, researchers often check if the data within each group resembles a bell curve.

Homogeneity of Variance

The spread (variance) of scores should be roughly equal across all groups. ANOVA is robust to minor violations, provided sample sizes are equal.

Independence

Data points must not influence each other. If the same student is tested multiple times, you must use a Repeated-Measures ANOVA instead.

Stage 04

Variance Decomposition

ANOVA answers our question by splitting the total variation in the data into two components. Look at the canvas to see these distances visualized.

  • Between-Group Variance (Signal) The systematic variance associated with the independent variable. Measured by how far each group mean (black line) deviates from the Grand Mean (dashed line).
  • Within-Group Variance (Noise) The unsystematic variance or error. Measured by how much individual dots deviate from their own group mean.
Stage 05

The F-Statistic

ANOVA summarizes this decomposition into a single ratio, the F-statistic. It compares the size of the treatment effect to the size of the error.

$$F = \frac{\text{Between-Group Variance}}{\text{Within-Group Variance}}$$
Stage 06

Degrees of Freedom

Before we can calculate our true variance (Mean Squares) for the F-statistic, we need to divide our Sum of Squares by our Degrees of Freedom (df).

In statistics, df simply means how many values are still free to vary once a constraint has been imposed. Imagine picking 3 numbers that must average to 10. You can freely choose the first two (say, 8 and 15), but the third is locked (-13). You have 2 degrees of freedom.

In our specific teaching example, we have N = 36 total students spread across k = 3 groups:

  • dfbetween = k - 1 = 3 - 1 = 2 Once the overall Grand Mean is set, only 2 of our 3 group means are free to vary.
  • dfwithin = N - k = 36 - 3 = 33 Within each group, the students' scores must average out to their respective group mean. We lose 1 degree of freedom per group constraint.
  • dftotal = N - 1 = 36 - 1 = 35 If we only knew the Grand Mean, 35 out of the 36 students' scores would be free to vary.
Stage 07

A Worked Example

Let’s bring this together using the data currently on display. We divide our raw distances (Sum of Squares, SS) by our degrees of freedom to get the Mean Squares (MS), which act as our final variance values.

From The Displayed Scores

Group means: A = 80, B = 68, C = 56. Grand mean = 68. Each group has n = 12, so N = 36.

Between-Group Signal

SSbetween = 12[(80 - 68)2 + (68 - 68)2 + (56 - 68)2] = 3456

Within-Group Noise

SSwithin = 132 + 132 + 132 = 396

Applying Degrees of Freedom

dfbetween = 2; dfwithin = 33.
MSbetween = 3456 / 2 = 1728.
MSwithin = 396 / 33 = 12.
F = 1728 / 12 = 144.

Stage 08

Reading the ANOVA Table

When you run ANOVA in software like R, SPSS, or Python, the output is standardized into this table format.

Interact with the table in the visual panel: Tap or hover over the column headers (SS, df, MS, F, p) and the rows to understand exactly what each number represents and how they mathematically connect.

The Conclusion:

Because our p-value (< 0.001) is less than our threshold (0.05), we reject the null hypothesis. We conclude that at least one teaching method has a significantly different mean score.

Stage 09

How Big Is The Effect?

A significant p-value tells us the result is likely real, but not necessarily if it's meaningful. A tiny signal can be highly significant if you have 10,000 students. For practical meaning, we measure the Effect Size.

Eta-Squared ($\eta^2$)

Eta-squared shows the proportion of the total variation in scores that is directly associated with the teaching method. We calculate it using the Sum of Squares from the table:

$\eta^2 = \frac{SS_{between}}{SS_{total}} = \frac{3456}{3852} = 0.90$

About 90% of the variance is explained by the group differences.

Notice the new column added to the ANOVA table in the visual panel. Depending on your software, this might be reported as Eta Squared, Partial Eta Squared, or $R^2$.

Stage 10

Post-Hoc Testing

A significant ANOVA is an omnibus test—it tells you a difference exists, but not where. Is A different from B? Is B different from C?

The Multiple Comparisons Problem

If you run three separate t-tests, your 5% chance of a false positive (Type I error) compounds. The more tests you run, the higher the Family-Wise Error Rate.

To follow up safely, we use Post-Hoc tests like Tukey's HSD. Under its assumptions, Tukey's controls the family-wise error rate at the chosen alpha level across the full set of pairwise comparisons. Look at the visual panel for the breakdown.

Stage 11

Enter Two-Way ANOVA

So far, we've used a One-Way ANOVA to ask a single question: Does the teaching method matter? But reality is complex.

Let's extend our example. We take our original three teaching methods (A, B, and C) and introduce a second factor: the student's Sleep Level (High vs. Low).

Two-Way ANOVA now asks three questions at once: Does teaching method matter? Does sleep matter? And crucially, does the effect of one depend on the other? We are no longer comparing only 3 group means, but 6 distinct cells (3 methods × 2 sleep levels).

This allows us to explore how both variables simultaneously influence test scores.

Stage 12

Cells & Marginal Means

To understand a two-way design, it helps to see how the data is organized. Look at this numeric structure for our extended teaching example:

  • Cell Means (The middle grid) A cell mean is the average score for one exact combination—like the students who received Method A AND High Sleep (82). Our design has 6 distinct cells.
  • Marginal Means (The outer edges) A marginal mean averages across one factor while ignoring the other. The marginal mean for Method A (72) combines both sleep levels. "Main effects" look only at these outer marginal means.
Stage 13

The Interaction Effect

If the gap between methods stays consistent regardless of sleep, the lines in our plot will be roughly parallel. That suggests no interaction.

But if the lines are not parallel, it suggests an interaction. Crossing lines are not technically required for an interaction, but this example uses a strong crossing pattern to make the concept visually obvious: the effect of Method completely flips depending on Sleep.

How to Read the Interaction Plot

  1. Step 1: Check for parallel lines. If lines are roughly parallel, there is likely no interaction. If they cross or clearly change relative position, an interaction is present.
  2. Step 2: Look at overall height. If one line tends to sit higher on average, that suggests a main effect of Sleep.
  3. Step 3: Look at how the ranking changes. Notice which method is best under High Sleep, and then notice how that changes under Low Sleep. Here, the best method completely reverses!
Stage 14

Building the ANOVA Table

How do we get from our six cells to the statistical output? We partition the total variation into four distinct buckets:

1
Method (Main Effect) Variation among the three Method marginal means (72 vs 70 vs 68). It asks if one teaching method is better on average.
2
Sleep (Main Effect) Variation between the two Sleep marginal means (74 vs 66). It asks if well-rested students do better on average.
3
Method × Sleep (Interaction) The leftover structured variance when the gaps between methods change across sleep levels. For High Sleep, A beats C by +16 points. But for Low Sleep, C beats A by +8 points.
4
Error (Within-Cell Noise) The natural random noise of individual students within each of the six specific cells.

To turn these buckets into our final results, each source gets its own Sum of Squares (SS). We divide each SS by its degrees of freedom (df) to get a Mean Square (MS). Finally, we compare each effect's MS to the Error MS to produce an F-ratio.

Why Averages Can Hide The Truth

Notice that our Method marginal means are quite close (72, 70, 68). Because the crossover pattern partly cancels out when averaged across sleep levels, the main effect of Method looks modest. But the interaction is large because the best method changes completely depending on sleep! This is why we always read the interaction row first.

Stage 15

Reading the Two-Way Table

Notice how the ANOVA table expands in the visual panel. Our single "Between Groups" signal is now cleanly split into three specific rows: Method, Sleep, and Method × Sleep.

Correct Reading Order

  1. Read the interaction row first. If the interaction is significant, the effect of one factor depends on the other.
  2. Be careful with main effects. If the interaction is significant, interpreting the main effect of Method or Sleep on its own can be highly misleading.
  3. Then read the main effects. Look at Method and Sleep to see if they matter on average across all conditions.

Our Example's Conclusion: The Interaction is highly significant (p < 0.001). This confirms what we visually saw—the lines clearly cross between Method B and Method C. Method A provides a large advantage for well-rested students, but Method C becomes the best option for tired students. The main effects (Method and Sleep) are also significant, but the interaction tells us the true story of how they combine.

Stage 16

Caveats & Recap

ANOVA is a beautiful, mathematically elegant tool for partitioning variance. But it is only a model of reality.

Assumptions are Key

ANOVA is robust, but severe violations of normality or independence will produce a completely meaningless F-statistic. Always check your residuals.

Look at the Data

Never rely solely on the p-value, tables, or bar charts. Plot the raw data (like our dot plot), check the boxplots, and ensure extreme outliers aren't driving the omnibus result.

In one sentence: ANOVA tests if group differences are real enough to stand out above the normal noise in the data.
