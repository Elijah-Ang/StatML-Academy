Stage 02

The Straight-Line Trap (LDA)

The simplest approach is Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA). It draws a straight boundary to separate the groups.

To do this, LDA calculates the pooled average shape of all the data and forces both groups to share that exact same covariance matrix ($\hat{\Sigma}_{shared}$).

The Limitation: By forcing the shapes to be identical, LDA misclassifies points near the edges. It completely ignores the unique diagonal stretch of the Pears.
Stage 03

The QDA Shift

Quadratic Discriminant Analysis (QDA) drops the "same shape" rule. It lets every class have its own unique personality.

Instead of a rigid jump rope, QDA acts more like a flexible hula hoop. The model looks at the generated points and empirically estimates a distinct covariance matrix ($\hat{\Sigma}_k$) for each group.

Watch the canvas: the ellipses now accurately wrap around the true scatter of each distinct class.

Stage 04

Anatomy of a Learned Class

To learn these shapes, QDA calculates three key statistics directly from the sample data you generated:

1. Prior ( $\pi_k$ )
The baseline probability based on sample sizes: $N_k / N_{total}$.

2. Estimated Mean ( $\hat{\mu}_k$ )
The empirical center coordinate of the class cloud.

3. Estimated Covariance ( $\hat{\Sigma}_k$ )
The shape, width, and tilt of the cloud computed from the points. We also add a tiny regularization ridge to ensure numerical stability!

This allows QDA to model the data as separate, multivariate normal distributions without assuming they match.

Stage 05

Testing with Smart Distance

When a new fruit arrives, QDA uses a shape-aware distance metric to see which cloud it fits best.

$$ \delta_k(x) = -\frac{1}{2} \ln |\hat{\Sigma}_k| - \frac{1}{2}(x-\hat{\mu}_k)^T \hat{\Sigma}_k^{-1} (x-\hat{\mu}_k) + \ln(\pi_k) $$

The Discriminant Function scores each class. The middle term measures distance along the group's natural stretch. The first term penalizes overly wide clouds, and the last term boosts common classes.

Try it: Tap and drag anywhere on the canvas to move the test probe point. Watch the posterior probabilities update dynamically in the tooltip.
Stage 06

The Curved Boundary

To make a final decision, QDA simply calculates $\delta_k(x)$ for both Apples and Pears across the entire space and maps out where the scores tie.

Because the covariance matrices $\hat{\Sigma}_k$ are different, the $(x-\mu)^2$ terms don't cancel out. The math natively produces a Quadratic Boundary that elegantly bends to protect the uniquely shaped groups.

Stage 07

The Cost of Flexibility

If QDA is so smart, why use LDA at all? The answer is the Bias-Variance Tradeoff and parameter complexity.

For $K$ classes and $p$ features, let's look at what the models must estimate from the data:

$Kp + \frac{p(p+1)}{2}$ LDA Parameters (Means + 1 Shared Covariance)
$Kp + K\frac{p(p+1)}{2}$ QDA Parameters (Means + K Covariances)

Estimating a unique covariance matrix for every class requires a lot of data. If your dataset is small or has many features, QDA's flexibility becomes a liability—it will start hallucinating curves out of noise (overfitting).

Stage 08

Synthesis

Generative models don't just guess lines; they map the structural landscape of the data.

QDA reigns supreme when classes naturally behave differently—when one is tight and round, and another is wide and stretched.

  • ✓ Best for: Large datasets, differing group shapes, nonlinear natural boundaries.
  • ✓ Assumption: Features follow a multivariate normal distribution within classes.
  • ✓ Mechanics: Computes empirical class covariances to enable a curved decision surface based on Mahalanobis distance and priors.

By letting each class keep its unique shape, QDA transforms classification from drawing rigid lines into understanding probabilities in space.
