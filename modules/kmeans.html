Unsupervised Learning

K-Means Clustering

A live, interactive guide to finding hidden structure in messy data. At its core, K-Means tries to minimize the total squared distance from points to their assigned centroids.

💡 Takeaway: Data starts as a scattered collection of observations with no inherent group labels.
Stage 02

The Blind Guess (Initialization)

We must choose how many groups (K) we want. But the algorithm has to start somewhere. It must make a blind guess.

In this demo, we use Random Partition: assigning every point to a random cluster and finding the center of those random teams.

Note: Another common method is Forgy (picking random data points as initial centers). Real-world applications mostly use k-means++ to smartly space out the initial guesses.

💡 Takeaway: K-Means cannot learn until it makes an initial, often terrible, guess at the cluster centers (centroids).
Stage 03

Nearest Neighbor Assignment

Every data point ignores its random assignment, measures the straight-line (Euclidean) distance to all current centroids, and joins the closest centroid for this round.

Dist = √[ (x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)² ]

Hover over the points on the canvas to see their exact distance to their newly chosen centroid.

💡 Takeaway: Points greedily choose the closest center to minimize their individual distance.
Stage 04

Recentering

The points have formed groups, but the original guess (the star) is no longer in the true center of its new team.

The algorithm calculates the mean (average) position of all points in a cluster, and the centroid slides exactly to that mathematical center of mass.

💡 Takeaway: Centroids dynamically update to reflect the actual center of the points assigned to them.
Stage 05

The Convergence Loop

Because the centroids moved, the boundaries changed. A point that was in the Blue cluster might now be closer to the Red centroid.

We loop Steps 3 and 4: Re-assign, Re-center, Re-assign, Re-center. Use the Step button to advance manually. When no points swap teams, the algorithm has converged.

💡 Takeaway: The algorithm loops until stable. It is guaranteed to converge, though not always to the perfect global solution.
Stage 06

Measuring Tightness (WCSS)

How good are these clusters? We measure the Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS). It is the sum of the squared distances from every point to its assigned centroid.

Look at the Status Strip above the canvas. Lower WCSS means tighter, denser clusters. We want this number to be as small as possible.

💡 Takeaway: WCSS is the score K-Means tries to minimize. Smaller means tighter clusters.
Stage 07

The Elbow Heuristic

How do we choose K? We compute K-Means for K=1, 2, 3, 4, 5... and plot the final WCSS for each.

The chart on the right is a fast demo estimate based on repeated runs using the current data. We look for the "Elbow" — the point where adding more clusters yields diminishing returns.

Warning: This is just a heuristic. It doesn't magically reveal a single unquestionable "correct" K. Real datasets are messy and often do not produce a clean bend.

💡 Takeaway: The Elbow method helps guess the optimal K, but human judgment is still required.
Stage 08

The Scaling Trap

Age is a small number (20-60). Spending is a massive number ($100-$10,000). Because K-Means uses raw geometric distance, the Y-axis (Spending) completely swallows the X-axis.

Look at the canvas: this is a demo visualization of scale imbalance. Without standardization, the data visually forms a thin vertical line, and the algorithm essentially ignores Age entirely!

Standardization doesn't magically change the dataset's meaning; it simply adjusts the feature scale before clustering so all columns contribute equally to the distance.

💡 Takeaway: Always Standardize/Scale your data (e.g., StandardScaler) so all features contribute equally to the distance.
Stage 09

Limitations & Outliers

K-Means is incredibly fast, but rigid. It assumes clusters are roughly circular (spherical) and equally sized.

Furthermore, because it calculates averages, it is highly sensitive to Outliers. An outlier has been added to the canvas—watch how it physically drags its centroid away from the true cluster mass.

💡 Takeaway: K-Means struggles with complex shapes and outliers. Data preparation is 90% of the battle.
