Stage 01

The Raw Data

Correlation is a statistical technique used to answer one big question: When one thing changes, does another thing also change?

To find out, we need paired observations. For example, let's look at the relationship between hours studied and test scores for six students.

Stage 02

Direction and Form

The first thing we look for in a scatterplot is the direction and form of the relationship.

If the dots rise from left to right, we have a Positive Correlation. As study hours go up, test scores also go up.

The "Walking Friends" metaphor: Think of two friends. If they both walk forward together, that's a positive correlation. If one walks forward while the other walks backward, that's negative.

We also check the form. Pearson correlation specifically measures linear relationships—meaning the dots tend to cluster around a straight line.

Stage 03

Measuring Strength: r

We quantify this relationship using Pearson's correlation coefficient, written as r.

The value of r always falls between -1.00 and +1.00.

  • r = +1.0 : Perfect positive correlation
  • r = -1.0 : Perfect negative correlation
  • r = 0.0 : No linear correlation

Stage 04

Formal Hypotheses

In statistics, we use a sample to make guesses about an entire population. To test if a correlation is "real", we set up formal hypotheses.

Null Hypothesis (H₀): ρ = 0

There is no linear correlation between the variables in the population. Any observed relationship is just random chance.

Alternative Hypothesis (H₁): ρ ≠ 0

There is a significant linear relationship between the two variables.

We use the Greek letter rho (ρ) for the population, and r for our sample.

Stage 05

How it works: Covariability

How does the math actually figure out if they move together? It looks at the means.

r =
Σ((X - X̄)(Y - Ȳ))
√[Σ(X - X̄)² × Σ(Y - Ȳ)²]

Translation: The sum of how they vary together, divided by the total of how they vary individually.

We draw a vertical line for the average of X, and a horizontal line for the average of Y. This creates four quadrants.

If points mostly fall in the Top-Right (both above average) and Bottom-Left (both below average), they are moving together. The formula multiplies their distances from the mean. Positive × Positive = Positive. Negative × Negative = Positive!

Stage 06

Assumptions: Paired & Linear

Pearson's correlation is powerful, but you must check two critical assumptions before trusting the math:

  • Paired Numeric Data: Both variables must be numeric, and each X must belong to the exact same subject as Y.
  • Linearity: The relationship must be relatively straight.

Imagine a scenario where cramming too much causes sleep deprivation, hurting test performance. Scores rise initially, then fall.

Stage 07

Assumption Check: Outliers

Another critical assumption is that there are no extreme outliers.

Because the formula squares the distances from the mean, a single point far away from the rest can act like a magnet, drastically altering the slope and the r value.

Watch how a single anomalous student (who studied 12 hours but scored 30) pulls the trendline down, severely weakening our strong positive correlation.

Stage 09

The Golden Rule: Causation

We found a statistically significant relationship. But how far can we stretch our interpretation?

Interpretation:

"There was a significant positive correlation between hours studied and test score, r = --, --. Students who studied more tended to score higher."

However, we cannot say that studying caused the score to increase.

Correlation does not equal causation. Two variables might move together because of a third, confounding variable (e.g., Ice cream sales and sunburns both rise together, but the sun causes both).

Stage 10

Limitations & Summary

To recap, correlation is an observational tool, not an experimental proof.

  • r measures linear tightness (from -1 to +1).
  • Check the plot: r is blind to curves and highly sensitive to outliers.
  • Significance vs. Size: A significant p-value means the relationship is likely real, but r tells you how strong it is.
  • Pearson vs. Spearman: Use Pearson for continuous data. Switch to Spearman if your data is ranked/ordinal or has a monotonic non-linear relationship.
  • No Causal Claims: It is only evidence of an association.

When you encounter a correlation, always ask: What does the scatterplot look like, and could a hidden variable be driving the pattern?
